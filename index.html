Library of Proof Calculi

Many proof calculi appearing in papers on proof theory are not spelled out in the paper (e.g. only available in a referenced textbook), or glossed over via "we use the usual axioms and rules of derivation." This is a collection of explicitly defined finitary proof calculi appearing in literature on proof theory.

NF

Quine (1981)

W. V. O. Quine, Mathematical Logic (1981) 0-674-55451-5, sections 28--29

Similar to the system appearing under Predicate calculus. Additional axioms are proposed, but I will not include those slight modifications on this page. The original text had many shorthands, many of those have been reduced here.

This is a Hilbert-style system, so a proof is a sequence of formulae, each entry is either an axiom or follows from previous entries via a rule of inference.

Axioms

  • Universal closures of all tautologous formulae
  • Universal closures of all formulae of the form \(\forall a(\phi\rightarrow\psi)\rightarrow((\forall a\phi)\rightarrow(\forall a\psi)\)
  • Universal closures of all formulae of the form \(\phi\rightarrow\forall x(\phi)\), where \(x\) is not a free variable in \(\phi\)
  • Universal closures of all formulae of the form \(\forall x(\phi)\rightarrow \phi'\), where \(\phi'\) is \(\phi\) but with all instances of \(x\) which are free replaced with a new variable symbol \(x'\)
  • Universal closures of formulae of the form \(\forall b_1,\ldots,b_n(b_1,\ldots,b_n\in V\rightarrow\exists c(\forall g(g\in c\leftrightarrow\exists h(g\in h\land\forall a(a\in h\rightarrow\phi)))\land c\in V))\) where \((b_i)_{i\lt n\in\mathbb N}\) enumerates the free variables of \(\phi\), which is a stratified formula. (Note: the most expansion of shorthands occurred when writing out this axiom. The most "important" free var of \(\phi\) should be \(a\))
  • Universal closures of formulae of the form \(x=x'\rightarrow(\phi\rightarrow\phi')\), where \(\phi'\) is \(\phi\) with all instances of \(x\) replaced with \(x'\)
  • Universal closures of formulae of the form \(\exists b\forall a(a\in b\leftrightarrow(a=a\land\phi))\), where the variable symbol \(a\) is not free in \(\phi\) nor equal to the variable symbol \(a\)

Rules of inference

  • Modus ponens

Takeuti (1987)

This is described as system LK. Old notation \(\supset\) used for material implication to avoid confusion with the sequent symbol \(\rightarrow\). Prime is used for successorship.

Axioms

  • \(\phi\rightarrow\phi\) for each formula \(\phi\)
  • \(\rightarrow s=s\) for any term \(s\)
  • s_1=t_1,\ldots,s_n=t_n\rightarrow f(s_1,\ldots,s_n)=f(t_1,\ldots,t_n) for any terms \(s_1,t_1,\ldots,s_n,t_n\) and any function symbol \(f\)
  • s_1=t_1,\ldots,s_n=t_n,R(s_1,\ldots,s_n)\rightarrow R(t_1,\ldots,t_n) for any terms \(s_1,t_1,\ldots,s_n,t_n\) and any relation symbol \(R\) (including \(=\))
  • \(s'=t'\rightarrow s=t\)
  • \(s'=0\rightarrow\)
  • \(\rightarrow s+0=s\)
  • \(\rightarrow s+t'=(s+t)'\)
  • \(\rightarrow s\cdot 0=0\)
  • \(\rightarrow s\cdot t'=s\cdot t+s\)

Let \(\phi_x(y)\) be the formula \(\phi\) with all instances of the var symbol \(x\) replaced by \(y\).

An underlined \(n\) is the term for the natural number \(n\) (this includes an underlined \(Sn\).) I have used \(S\) to denote the function symbol the source denotes \(\underline S\), in order to reserve underlined text for the preceding terms.

Axioms:

  • All "sententially valid formulae" (\(\phi\) where when \(\phi\)'s atomic subformulae are evaluated w.r.t. any assignment \(\{\chi\mid\chi\textrm{ atomic or of the form }\forall x\psi(x),\;\exists x\psi(x)\}\to\{\top,\bot\}\), \(\phi\) is true)
  • All formulae of the form \(\lnot\forall x(A)\lor A_x(t)\) or \(\lnot A_x(t)\lor\exists x(A)\)
  • \(\forall x(x=x)\)
  • \(\forall x\forall y(x=y\rightarrow y=x)\)
  • \(\forall x\forall y\forall z(x=y\land y=z\rightarrow x=z)\)
  • \(\forall x\forall y(x=y\rightarrow(F\rightarrow F_x(y)))\)
  • \(\forall x(\lnot\underline 0=Sx)\)
  • \(\forall x\forall y(Sx=Sy\rightarrow x=y)\)
  • \(\underline{Sn}=S\underline n\) for each \(n\in\mathbb N\)
  • The schema \((A_x(0)\land\forall y(A_x(y)\rightarrow A_x(Sy)))\rightarrow\forall xA\)

Rules of inference:

  • \(A,\lnot A\lor B\vdash B\) (modus ponens)
  • \(\lnot A\lor B\vdash\lnot A\lnot \forall xB\)
  • \(\lnot B\lor A\vdash\lnot\exists xB\lor A\)
Buchholz, Wainer (1987) Provably computable functions and the fast-growing hierarchy

Finite sequences of formulae \(A_1,\ldots,A_k\) are intended to mean the disjunction \(A_1\lor\ldots\lor A_k\). \(\Gamma,A\) is intended to mean \(\Gamma\cup\{A\}\), e.g. \(\Gamma,x=x\) is intended to mean \(\Gamma\cup\{x=x\}\).

Axioms:

  • \(\Gamma,\lnot A,A\) for every formula \(A\)
  • \(\Gamma,x=x\)
  • \(\Gamma,x\neq y,y=x\)
  • \(\Gamma,x\neq y,y\neq z,x=z\)
  • \(\Gamma,Sx\neq 0\)
  • \(\Gamma,Sx\neq Sy,x=y\)
  • \(\Gamma,x\neq y,Sx=Sy\)
  • (Similar axioms defining the behavior of elementary functions)
  • \(\Gamma,\lnot A(0),\exists x(A(x)\land\lnot A(Sx)),\forall xA(x)\) for every formula \(A\)

Rules of inference:

  • \begin{prooftree}\AxiomC{$\Gamma,A_0$} \AxiomC{$\Gamma,A_1$} \RightLabel{($\land$)} \BinaryInfC{$\Gamma,(A_0\land A_1)$}\end{prooftree}
  • \begin{prooftree}\AxiomC{$\Gamma,A_i$} \RightLabel{($\lor$)} \UnaryInfC{$\Gamma,(A_0\lor A_1)$}\end{prooftree}, i is one of 0 or 1
  • \begin{prooftree}\AxiomC{$\Gamma,A(x)$} \RightLabel{($\forall$)} \UnaryInfC{$\Gamma,\forall x A(x)$}\end{prooftree}, x not free in Gamma
  • \begin{prooftree}\AxiomC{$\Gamma,A(t)$} \RightLabel{($\exists$)} \UnaryInfC{$\Gamma,\exists x A(x)$}\end{prooftree}
  • \begin{prooftree}\AxiomC{$\Gamma,\lnot A$} \AxiomC{$\Gamma,A$} \RightLabel{(Cut)} \BinaryInfC{$\Gamma$}\end{prooftree}

Kunen, The Foundations of Mathematics (2007, draft version), section II.10

This is a Hilbert-style system, so a proof is a sequence of formulae, each entry is either an axiom or follows from previous entries via a rule of inference.

When \(\varphi\) is a formula with free variable \(x\) and \(\tau\) is a term, \(\varphi(x\rightsquigarrow\tau)\) is \(\varphi\) with all appearances of \(x\) replaced with \(\tau\).

Axioms:

  • Propositional tautologies
  • Formulae of the form \(\phi\rightarrow\forall x\phi\) where \(x\) not free in \(\phi\)
  • Formulae of the form \(\forall x(\phi\rightarrow\psi)\rightarrow((\forall x\phi)\rightarrow(\forall x\psi)\)
  • Formulae of the form \((\forall x\phi)\rightarrow\phi(x\rightsquigarrow\tau\), where \(x\) is free in \(\phi\) and \(\tau\) is any term
  • Formulae of the form \(\phi(x\rightsquigarrow\tau)\rightarrow\exists x\phi\), where \(x\) is free in \(\phi\) and \(\tau\) is any term
  • Formulae of the form \((\forall x\lnot\phi)\leftrightarrow\lnot\exists x\phi\)
  • \(x=x\)
  • \(x=y\leftrightarrow y=x\)
  • \(x=y\land y=z\rightarrow x=z\)
  • \(x_1=y_1\land\ldots\land x_n=y_n\rightarrow(f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=f(y_1,\ldots,y_n))\) for any \(n>0\), where \(f\) is an \(n\)-place function symbol in this language
  • \(x_1=y_1\land\ldots\land x_n=y_n\rightarrow(p(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\leftrightarrow p(y_1,\ldots,y_n))\) for any \(n>0\), where \(p\) is an \(n\)-place relation symbol in this language

Quine (1981)

W. V. O. Quine, Mathematical Logic (1981) 0-674-55451-5, sections 15--16

This is a Hilbert-style system, so a proof is a sequence of formulae, each entry is either an axiom or follows from previous entries via a rule of inference.

Axioms

  • Universal closures of all tautological formulae
  • Universal closures of all formulae of the form \(\forall a(\phi\rightarrow\psi)\rightarrow((\forall a\phi)\rightarrow(\forall a\psi)\)
  • Universal closures of all formulae of the form \(\phi\rightarrow\forall x(\phi)\), where \(x\) is not a free variable in \(\phi\)
  • Universal closures of all formulae of the form \(\forall x(\phi)\rightarrow \phi'\), where \(\phi'\) is \(\phi\) but with all instances of \(x\) which are free replaced with a new variable symbol \(x'\)

Rules of inference

  • Modus ponens
